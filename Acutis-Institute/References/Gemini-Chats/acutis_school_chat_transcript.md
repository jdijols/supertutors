### Assistant
Private, non-profit Catholic schools that are not owned or operated by a diocese or a religious order are commonly referred to as **Independent Catholic schools** (or private lay-led Catholic schools). 

While they maintain a strong Catholic identity and curriculum, their legal governance, financing, and daily operations are completely separate from the official Church hierarchy.

#### How They Work: The Governance Structure
Unlike traditional parochial schools (run by a parish) or diocesan schools (run by the local bishop), independent Catholic schools are structured like independent secular private schools:
* **Incorporation:** They are incorporated as independent **501(c)(3) non-profit organizations**. 
* **The Board of Trustees:** They are governed by a self-perpetuating lay board of directors or trustees. This board is entirely responsible for fundraising, purchasing property, hiring the Head of School, and managing the budget. 
* **Financing:** They receive zero financial subsidization or operational funding from the diocese. They rely strictly on tuition, endowments, and private donations.

#### Can They Legally Call Themselves "Catholic"?
This is where the intersection with the Church occurs. Under Catholic Canon Law (**Canon 803 §3**), no school may bear the title "Catholic School" without the explicit consent of the competent ecclesiastical authority (usually the local bishop). 

1.  **Recognized Independent Schools:** Many of these schools maintain a cooperative relationship with the local diocese. The bishop formally recognizes their Catholic identity, and they are allowed to use the name "Catholic." However, this is purely a spiritual and doctrinal recognition—the bishop has no legal ownership of the school's assets, land, or corporate entity.
2.  **Independent Apostolates:** Some schools operate completely outside of diocesan recognition. While their curriculum is deeply rooted in traditional Catholic theology, they may use names like "Academy" or "Institute" rather than explicitly branding as a diocesan-approved "Catholic School."

#### Prominent Networks and Associations
The growth of independent Catholic education has accelerated over the last few decades, leading to the creation of several prominent national networks:
* **The Chesterton Schools Network:** A rapidly growing network of independent, lay-led high schools focused on a classical curriculum (focusing on the arts, humanities, logic, and philosophy). Each school is a separate non-profit founded and run by local parents and community members, utilizing a shared curriculum model.
* **NAPCIS (National Association of Private, Catholic, and Independent Schools):** An accrediting body specifically designed for independent, lay-run Catholic schools. It helps these institutions maintain academic accreditation and strict adherence to the Magisterium (the teaching authority of the Church) without relying on diocesan administrative structures.
* **The Regina Academies:** A smaller, regional network of independent classical schools governed by lay boards, focusing on elementary and middle school education.

#### Examples of Independent Catholic Schools
These schools exist across the country. A few examples include:
* **St. Gabriel's Catholic School** (Austin, TX) – A private, non-profit, independent K-8 school governed by a lay Board of Trustees.
* **The Avalon School** (Bethesda, MD) – An independent boys' school offering a traditional education rooted in Catholic faith.
* **Holy Angels Academy** (Louisville, KY) – One of the oldest continuously operating independent lay-led Catholic schools in the US.
* **Everest Collegiate High School & Academy** (Clarkston, MI) – An independent school affiliated with a lay religious apostolate (Regnum Christi) rather than a diocese.

The short answer is **no, not automatically.** While you can completely control your school's civil entity name as a private 501(c)(3) non-profit, the use of the word **"Catholic"** in a school’s name is governed strictly by Catholic Canon Law, not civil law. 

To use that exact name legally under Church law, you have to cross two specific canonical hurdles.

#### 1. The "Catholic" Label Requires the Bishop's Permission
Under **Canon 803 §3** of the Code of Canon Law, the rule is absolute: 
> *"Even if it is in fact Catholic, no school is to bear the name Catholic school without the consent of the competent ecclesiastical authority."*

The "competent authority" is almost always the local Diocesan Bishop. Even if your curriculum is deeply traditional, your staff is entirely Catholic, and your board is comprised of faithful laypeople, you cannot legally market or name the institution a "Catholic School" unless the bishop issues a formal, written decree recognizing it as such. 

#### 2. Naming a School After a Saint
In Catholic tradition, naming a sacred space or an official apostolate (like a recognized school) after a canonized saint also requires ecclesiastical oversight. Because Carlo Acutis was formally canonized as a saint, his name is cleared for universal devotion. However, dedicating an institution to him under the banner of the Church still requires the local bishop's approval to ensure the saint's name is being used appropriately and in a setting that aligns with Church teaching.

#### What Happens If the Bishop Says No (or You Don't Ask)?
If you choose to operate completely independently without seeking formal diocesan recognition, you cannot use the phrase "Catholic School" in your official branding. 

However, independent lay groups do this all the time by adjusting their naming strategy. You can still explicitly teach the Catholic faith and root your culture in Saint Carlo's patronage; you just change the generic descriptor. 

* **If Recognized by the Bishop:** *St. Carlo Acutis Catholic School* or *Acutis Catholic High School*
* **If Operating Independently (No Formal Decree):** *The St. Carlo Acutis Academy* or *The Carlo Acutis Institute*

Many classical and independent schools choose the "Academy" path. They mention clearly in their mission statement and marketing materials that they offer an education *"rooted in the Catholic tradition"* or *"faithful to the Magisterium,"* which is entirely legal from both a civil and canonical standpoint, so long as the school doesn't claim the official corporate title of a "Catholic School."

#### The Recommended First Step
If you want the word "Catholic" in the name, your board's first major milestone needs to be setting up a meeting with the local ordinary (the bishop) or the diocesan Superintendent of Schools. You will want to bring:
* Your secular 501(c)(3) incorporation paperwork.
* A clear statement of faith and academic curriculum showing alignment with Catholic dogma.
* A written proposal outlining your operational independence (so the diocese knows they bear no financial liability for your school).
